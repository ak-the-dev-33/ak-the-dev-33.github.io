I started out the way most engineers do — curious about how things break, and more curious about how to put them back together better. Five years and a handful of enterprise platforms later, that curiosity has turned into a career spent building systems that hold up under real traffic, real deadlines, and real stakes: products used inside American Express, Verizon, and Societe Generale.

My engineering philosophy is simple — boring infrastructure, exciting product. The best systems are the ones nobody has to think about: predictable APIs, clean component architecture, deploys that don't require luck. I'd rather spend a week getting an abstraction right than a year paying interest on a wrong one.

Somewhere along the way, I noticed the gap between what college teaches and what the first day of a real engineering job actually demands — and decided to close it myself. That became BootChamps. Mentoring isn't a side project for me; it's the same instinct that makes me a better engineer, applied to people instead of systems — find where the gap is, then build the bridge.

// 05 — leadership & mentorship

I build engineers, not just software

Founding BootChamps came from a simple observation: the distance between "I finished a course" and "I'm ready for my first engineering job" is wider than it should be — and almost nobody is designing for that gap on purpose.

I treat teaching like I treat system design: start from the failure modes. Where do students actually get stuck? Where does confidence break before competence does? That's where I design structured roadmaps, run mock technical interviews, and build presentations that compress months of "figuring it out alone" into days of guided, project-based practice.

It's the work I'm proudest of — not because it's easier than enterprise engineering, but because the compounding return is different. A well-architected service serves requests. A well-mentored engineer goes on to build the next ten things.
